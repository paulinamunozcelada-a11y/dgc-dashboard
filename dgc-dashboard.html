From the newsletter

Investment Areas

The structural themes DGC tracks across editions where policy, capital, and unmet need are converging at the same time.

Healthcare

Community Health Workers

CHWs are the most underleveraged members of the healthcare workforce stack and the closest to the patient. Evidence shows CHWs working with Medicaid beneficiaries save $2,500 per enrollee annually. Medicare introduced its first CHW billing code in 2024. Capital is starting to follow.

Deals: Zocalo Health · Yuvo Health · Somethings

Healthcare

GLP-1 Access and Infrastructure

GLP-1 spending in Medicaid grew from $999M in 2019 to $8.6B in 2024. The BALANCE Model negotiated a $245/month price with Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk. The missing piece is the analytics and contracting infrastructure that makes GLP-1 ROI legible for payers before generics arrive.

Deals: Knownwell · Ilant Health · Benepass

Healthcare

Supervised Clinical AI

The real divide in AI therapy is supervised clinical AI vs. unregulated consumer chatbots. A Dartmouth RCT found users of a generative AI therapy tool experienced a 51% reduction in depression symptoms. The companies DGC finds most compelling keep humans in the loop by design.

Deals: Jimini Health · Blossom Health · Amigo AI · Triangle Health

Women's Health

Medicaid Doula Coverage

26+ states now reimburse doula services under Medicaid. Women enrolled who used a doula faced a 47% lower risk of C-section and a 29% lower risk of preterm birth. Medicaid funds 40% of all US births and nearly two-thirds of births of Black women.

Deals: Flourish Care · Malama Health · Nadia Care · SimpliFed

Workforce

Skilled Trades Surge

Four forces converging: AI buildout generating 4.7M construction jobs, reshoring policy, aging workforce, and semiconductor manufacturing. Electricians projected at +9.5% through 2034. The question is whether training pipelines can keep up with the demand surge.

Deals: Ultralight · Flashpass · Pelgo · Ando

Education

AI in the Classroom

Stanford reviewed 800+ AI education papers -- only 20 had strong enough evidence to draw conclusions. Purpose-built tutoring tools outperform general-purpose chatbots. The equity risk: under-resourced districts end up with the free version of AI that makes learning worse.

Deals: Subject · BoldVoice · Preply · Cloudforce

Fintech

Embedded Savings Infrastructure

Since 1989, a dollar in the S&P 500 grew 15x faster than a dollar tied to median wages. The barrier to wealth-building is structural, not behavioral. When saving is the default rather than opt-in, participation soars. The question is who builds those pipes for hourly and gig workers.

Deals: Vestwell · Ascent · Debbie

Healthcare

Value-Based Care + AI

AI changes the economics of VBC by enabling continuous monitoring, proactive outreach, and personalized care planning at scale. Chamber proved the model in cardiology with a 39% drop in heart failure hospitalizations. Similar plays are emerging in oncology, nephrology, and behavioral health.

Deals: Chamber · Atlas Oncology · Graici · Honest Health

The thesis

Capital allocation is social policy.

Every dollar deployed into a venture-backed company is a bet on who gets access to care, to work, to wealth, to opportunity. Do Good Capital covers capital allocation at the edges, where the biggest market opportunities and the greatest unmet needs are the same thing.

The contrarian view: AI is going to deepen income inequality. But AI will also make social safety net infrastructure cheaper and more scalable than ever before. The question is not whether AI is good or bad. It is whether the people building it are thinking about who it reaches. Most are not.

Impact and returns are not in tension. Reaching more people is how you make money. The biggest market opportunity in healthcare, workforce, and education is the population that traditional capital has systematically ignored.

How deals get selected

The access lens.

Every deal on this dashboard is filtered through one question: does this actually reach the patient, student, or worker who needs it most -- or does it stop at the commercially insured, the college-educated, the urban professional?

In-network is not the same as accessible

Medicaid reimbursement rates are a structural cap on unit economics. The access claim must hold up against the rate schedule, not just the insurance card.

Bipartisan, critical, honest

DGC does not paint policy as purely progressive or conservative. Readers should leave with better judgment, not borrowed conclusions.

Access alone is not enough

Clinical and economic outcomes must come first. The Midi Health case is a recurring DGC reference: a platform can expand access while delivering questionable value if the revenue model drifts from evidence-based care.
